A Note from the Author
Foreword

My fascination with computers began in the seventh grade — though you wouldn't have recognized what I was working with as a computer. My first machine wasn't electronic at all. It was a series of matchboxes that solved problems by moving colored dots of paper from one compartment to another, following very specific rules. Later, I scrounged enough transistors to solder together that fundamental building block of computation: the half adder.

A half adder is a circuit capable of adding two binary digits and producing a result and a carry-out. That carry-out feeds into the carry-in port of the next adder, and by stringing them together you can operate on numbers of any length. As the name suggests, though, a half adder doesn't support a carry-in on its own — you need two of them to make a full adder. These were the things I was turning over in my mind while other kids played baseball in the Arizona heat. It was 1969, and there really were no computers — not in any world most people could see. No one had encountered one except as a prop in the occasional science fiction film. But I was hooked. Even though my little machines didn't actually do anything, I could see the potential with total clarity.

When I got my hands on a surplus computer terminal — a briefcase unit with an acoustic coupler — I tied up the household phone every night for hours, dialing into the timeshare system at the local community college. My cousin still remembers coming over and finding me absorbed in the thing, hour after hour. He would ask what it was and what I was doing. I told him: "Someday, everyone is going to have one of these in their house."

He told me recently, "I thought you were completely crazy." Then he paused. "But now I carry one in my pocket."

I have always lived in the future.

By the time Joseph Weizenbaum's Computer Power and Human Reason was published, I had already been wrestling with the ideas of artificial intelligence for years. Building a machine that could learn sat right at the edge of our imagination in those days. The early attempts were simple and wonderful — programs like Animal and ELIZA.

Animal would ask you to think of a creature and then try to guess what you had in mind. When you first ran it, the program knew nothing, so you had to tell it the answer. But it remembered. Each time it guessed wrong, it would ask: "What is the difference between your animal and the one I guessed?" It stored that distinction and used it the next time. After enough sessions, it could play something close to twenty questions and identify quite a respectable menagerie.

ELIZA, which Weizenbaum discusses at length in his book, was different in character. It was designed to simulate a psychiatrist, using the simplest pattern-matching tools imaginable. It understood nothing — but if it detected the word "mother" in something you typed, it would reply: "Tell me about your mother." Then it would go back to scanning for the next keyword. The simulation was shallow beyond description, and yet people found it uncanny. Some found it comforting. That was the thing that stopped Weizenbaum cold, and rightly so.

Because while the rest of us were absorbed in the puzzle of how to make machines appear to think, Weizenbaum was looking further ahead. He recognized the implications of surrendering the responsibility for thinking to a machine — of what it would mean, not just technically but morally, to hand over judgment. He saw the danger in the trick before anyone else thought to ask whether it was dangerous.

And now, fifty years later, we are further down that road than even he anticipated. I was, among my cohort of compulsive programmers, almost alone in having read him. That book marked me. It asked questions I couldn't stop thinking about, and it demanded a kind of moral seriousness about the machines we were building that the culture around me was not particularly interested in. This book is my attempt, half a century on, to return to those questions — and to pay a small tribute to the man who saw it all coming, long before the rest of us were willing to look.

— The Author, 2026
