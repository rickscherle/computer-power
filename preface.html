Preface

The Prophecy That Came True

In 1966, Joseph Weizenbaum, a professor of computer science at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology, built a program he called ELIZA. It was, by the standards of what would follow, a simple thing — a pattern-matching system that could hold a rudimentary conversation by reflecting a user's words back to them in the form of questions. He designed it partly as a demonstration, partly as a provocation, and named it after the flower girl in Shaw's Pygmalion, who is taught to speak in ways that disguise what she is.

What happened next shook him so profoundly that he spent the following decade trying to articulate why.

People talked to ELIZA as if it were real. Not as a game, not as an experiment, but with the earnestness of genuine disclosure. His own secretary, who had watched him build the system and knew precisely what it was, asked him to leave the room so that she could speak to it in private. Psychiatrists wrote to him suggesting that a scaled-up version of ELIZA could serve as a therapeutic tool — that the program might provide, cheaply and at scale, the kind of support that human therapists could not. Weizenbaum was not flattered. He was appalled. The psychiatrists had seen a machine produce the surface features of empathic listening and concluded that something like empathy must therefore be present. They had mistaken the appearance of understanding for understanding itself. And this mistake, Weizenbaum believed, was not merely an error about a computer program. It was a symptom of something deeply wrong in the way a technological civilization had come to think about the relationship between human beings and the tools they build.

He wrote Computer Power and Human Reason to explain why. It was published in 1976, when the personal computer did not yet exist, when the internet was a military research project known to almost no one, when artificial intelligence was a field of extravagant promise and limited achievement. In that context, Weizenbaum's warnings must have seemed to many readers like the anxieties of a man who had looked further ahead than the evidence warranted. His central arguments — that computers were not neutral tools but active shapers of human thought; that the relentless quantification of experience was destroying forms of knowing that could not be recovered once lost; that there were decisions about human lives which should never be delegated to machines, regardless of their technical capability — were received with respect, and filed away, and largely ignored.

The world he warned about is the world we now inhabit.

· · ·

This is not a book about artificial intelligence in the technical sense. It does not contain a survey of machine learning architectures or a history of neural networks or an assessment of which benchmarks the latest models have surpassed. Those books exist in abundance, and they are useful, and this is not one of them. This is a book about what we have done — what we are doing — with the power that those systems represent, and about the human consequences of choices that were made without adequate thought about their human consequences.

It is, in this sense, a continuation of Weizenbaum's project. But where he was warning, this book is reckoning. The future he described has arrived. The questions he asked — questions about responsibility, about the limits of calculation, about what it means to be human in a world increasingly administered by machines — are no longer hypothetical. They are live, urgent, and in many cases being answered badly by default, in the absence of the deliberate public conversation that should have happened long ago and still has not.

Weizenbaum's title was a provocation: Computer Power and Human Reason. The conjunction was the argument. These two things, he was suggesting, stand in tension — not because computers are evil or reason is fragile, but because there is a kind of power that, when it becomes dominant, tends to crowd out and eventually to discredit forms of knowing and judging that cannot be expressed in its terms. He called it instrumental reason: the reduction of all questions to questions of means, the assumption that if something can be optimized it should be, the progressive colonization of human life by the logic of calculation. He was not opposed to calculation. He was opposed to the imperialism of calculation — to its expansion into domains where it does not belong and cannot, by its nature, do justice to what is at stake.

That imperialism is now total, or nearly so. It has penetrated medicine and law and education and politics and intimate life. It has produced systems of extraordinary sophistication and systems of extraordinary harm, often simultaneously. It has generated wealth on a scale that previous generations could not have imagined, and concentrated that wealth in ways that previous generations would have found obscene. It has, in short, done what powerful technologies always do: it has revealed the values of the civilization that built it.

Those values are not, on inspection, entirely flattering.

· · ·

A word about what this book is not. It is not a call for the abolition of artificial intelligence, or for the reversal of the digital transformation of modern life, or for any other program of technological retrenchment that is as politically impossible as it is probably undesirable. Weizenbaum was not a Luddite, and neither is this book. The tools are extraordinary. Some of what they have made possible is genuinely good — the acceleration of drug discovery, the detection of diseases that human eyes miss, the extension of education and communication to people who would otherwise have been excluded from them. The case for these applications is real and should be taken seriously by anyone who wants to be taken seriously in return.

But the fact that a technology can be used well does not mean that it is being used well. And the fact that some applications of a technology are clearly beneficial does not mean that all applications are, or that the technology's expansion into every domain of human life is therefore justified. These are distinctions that the technology industry has worked hard to collapse — and they are distinctions that must be reinstated clearly and held firmly.

What this book calls for is something more modest than abolition and more serious than regulation in its current form: a principled account of which decisions about human beings should remain in human hands, why they should remain there, and what it would take, institutionally and politically and culturally, to keep them there. Weizenbaum provided the philosophical foundations for such an account. This book attempts to build on them — to take those foundations and test them against the specific, concrete, documented reality of what has happened in the fifty years since he wrote.

· · ·

I am aware of the peculiarity of writing a book that argues for the irreducible importance of human judgment in a moment when the machines being criticized are capable of producing prose that resembles, from the outside, the product of human judgment. I do not think this peculiarity defeats the argument. I think it sharpens it.

The question this book asks is not whether machines can produce outputs that look like the products of thought and care and conscience. Clearly they can. The question is whether those outputs are the products of thought and care and conscience — and whether, in the domains that matter most to human life, the distinction between the appearance and the reality can be safely ignored. Weizenbaum's answer was no. This book's answer is no. And the fact that the question is now harder to answer than it was in 1976 — that the appearances are more convincing, that the boundary between simulation and understanding is more contested — makes the answer not less important but more so.

Weizenbaum closed his book with a passage that has stayed with me since I first encountered it. Science, he wrote, can give us facts. It cannot give us values. The decision about what to do with our power — which uses of it to embrace, which to refuse, which to surround with the kinds of limits that reflect our deepest commitments to one another — is not a scientific question. It is a human question, in the fullest sense: a question about what we are and what we want to become. That question has never been more urgent. It has never been more neglected. And it has never been more available for the taking, if we choose to take it.

This book is an argument that we should.

— The Author, 2026
