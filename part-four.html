Part IV

Who Is Responsible

This is the part of the book I find most uncomfortable to write. Because it's not just about institutions or industries or systems in the abstract. It's about people. Specific people, making specific choices, in full knowledge of the consequences. And some of those people are people I know. Some of them are people I've admired. Some of them, in some small ways, is me.

One of the things I've noticed about conversations in the technology industry — and I've been in a lot of them, over a long time — is how skilled we became at distributing responsibility until it disappeared. The engineer who built the model wasn't responsible for how it was deployed. The executive who deployed it wasn't responsible for the policy environment that made that deployment attractive. The regulator wasn't responsible for the gaps in the legislation. The legislator wasn't responsible for the pace of technical change. Everyone was doing their job. No one was responsible for the outcome.

I've heard this reasoning applied to surveillance systems, to hiring algorithms, to social media recommendation engines, to predictive policing tools. It always sounds plausible in the individual case. It always adds up, in the aggregate, to the same thing: a world full of harmful systems and an absence of anyone who will own the harm.

Part Four is my attempt to name who is actually responsible, and what responsibility would require of them. The first chapter looks at the scientific and engineering community — my community — and the long tradition of declining to ask what the work is for. The second looks at the failure of governance, which is real and serious, but which I also think gets used as an excuse more than it deserves to. The third steps back and asks the deeper question: whether an industry built on optimization is structurally capable of recognizing the things it can't optimize — and what it would take to change that.

I am not interested in blame for its own sake. I'm interested in accountability, which is different — it points forward rather than backward. What would it mean to actually accept responsibility for this? What would have to change? Who would have to decide to change it?

Those are the questions I want to sit with in this part of the book.
