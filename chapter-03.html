Part One  ·  Chapter Three

The New Compulsives

I was one of the people Weizenbaum was writing about.

Not proudly, exactly. But honestly. The compulsive programmer he described in his book — the one who sat at the terminal for days at a time, who found in the machine a kind of total absorption that nothing else provided, who was, in some important sense, more comfortable with the system than with the people around him — I recognized that person. I had been that person, in my own version of it. Most of the people I knew in those years had been that person, to one degree or another.

What Weizenbaum understood, and what I didn't fully appreciate until much later, was that this wasn't just a personality quirk or a professional hazard. It was a signal. The machine had properties — its responsiveness, its consistency, its immediate and legible feedback — that happened to map onto human psychological vulnerabilities in ways that made it unusually hard to put down. That wasn't a design choice. In 1976 it was just a byproduct. A side effect of what the machine was.

Then someone realized you could do it on purpose.

· · ·

The transition happened gradually enough that I almost missed it. I was paying attention to the wrong things — to processor speeds and memory capacities and programming languages — while a different kind of engineering was quietly taking over. Not hardware engineering. Human engineering.

The people who built the early social platforms were, a lot of them, people I knew or knew of. Smart, technically excellent, genuinely excited about what the internet could become. And in the beginning the products they built were exactly what they said they were: tools for connection. Ways to find people, share things, stay in touch. I used them. I thought they were remarkable.

At some point — and I don't think there was a single moment, more like a gradual drift — the question changed. It stopped being how do we help people connect? and became how do we keep people on the platform? Those sound similar. They are not similar at all.

Keeping people on the platform is an optimization problem. And it turns out the research literature on behavioral psychology — Skinner boxes, variable-ratio reinforcement schedules, the neurochemistry of social approval and social anxiety — gave you a pretty clear map of how to solve it. You don't make the experience satisfying, because satisfaction leads to putting the phone down. You make it compelling. Unresolved. Just-one-more-scroll. You give people a little hit of dopamine — a like, a comment, a follower — on an unpredictable schedule, because unpredictable rewards are more addictive than predictable ones. You remove the natural stopping points. You make the feed infinite.

Sean Parker, one of the original architects of Facebook, described this publicly in 2017 with a candor that I found both admirable and deeply unsettling. The question they had asked themselves, he said, was: how do we consume as much of your time and conscious attention as possible? The answer was a vulnerability exploit. He used those words. A vulnerability exploit — on human psychology, at scale, deployed in a product used by billions of people, including children.

He said it like he was confessing. But the product kept running.

· · ·

I want to talk about what this has done to young people, because I think it's the most serious consequence of all of this and the one that gets the least serious attention.

Adolescence is not just a period of life. It's a developmental project. The things that happen during it — forming an identity, learning to navigate genuine social complexity, discovering what you believe and who you want to be, tolerating boredom and uncertainty and the slow accumulation of experience — these things require specific conditions. They require friction. They require time that isn't filled. They require the genuine unpredictability of real human relationships, as opposed to the managed, legible, quantified social dynamics of a platform.

What we handed an entire generation instead was a device engineered to be maximally compelling, combined with a social environment in which their status, their belonging, and their sense of self were being continuously measured and displayed in public numbers. Follower counts. Like totals. The architecture of a slot machine applied to the developmental period when human beings are most vulnerable to it.

The results are in, and they are not ambiguous. Rates of anxiety and depression among adolescents — particularly girls — started rising sharply in the early 2010s, which is precisely when smartphone ownership became near-universal in this age group. Experimental studies in which participants were randomly assigned to reduce social media use have shown measurable improvements in wellbeing. The US Surgeon General issued advisories on both the youth mental health crisis and the loneliness epidemic, and both of them pointed in the same direction. The platforms that were supposed to connect us had made us lonelier. The tools designed to maximize engagement had hollowed out the thing engagement was supposed to be about.

I find this genuinely heartbreaking. Not as an abstraction — as a fact about specific human beings whose developmental years were spent in a system optimized for someone else's quarterly revenue.

· · ·

The Facebook Files — a series of internal documents leaked to the Wall Street Journal in 2021 — revealed that Facebook's own researchers had conducted studies on Instagram's effects on teenage girls. The findings were damning. Girls who used Instagram heavily reported worse body image, more depression, more anxiety, more suicidal ideation than girls who didn't. The researchers knew this. They documented it carefully. They recommended changes.

The product was not changed.

The decision about what to do with that research was made by people who had seen it and understood it. They chose engagement. They chose it while knowing what they were choosing. That is not a software failure. That is a moral failure, made by specific human beings who bear responsibility for it.

I am not letting the rest of us off the hook, though. We knew too, in a general way. The concern wasn't hidden. Researchers had been publishing on it for years. Parents were watching their kids. We just — didn't make it stop. Collectively, we accepted the terms. We kept the apps on our phones and our children's phones. We fed the system. I fed the system. That's part of the story too.

· · ·

There's a defense of all this that I've heard many times, usually from people in the industry, sometimes from libertarian-leaning commentators, occasionally from people who should know better. It goes: these are tools, and adults are free to use tools as they see fit, and any attempt to regulate or restrict that is paternalism.

I used to find this argument at least partially compelling. I don't anymore.

A slot machine is also a tool. We regulate slot machines. We restrict where they can be placed, who can use them, how they can be advertised. We do this because we recognize that the design of a slot machine is specifically intended to overwhelm the user's capacity for autonomous choice — to operate below the level of deliberate decision, exploiting the architecture of the reward system in ways that make "free choice" a somewhat theoretical concept. Nobody who has studied the neuroscience of compulsive gambling thinks the person feeding quarters into a machine at two in the morning is freely choosing to do so in any meaningful sense of the phrase.

Social media platforms are slot machines that got inside our social lives. They exploit the same psychological mechanisms. They were designed to. The freedom argument doesn't fail because freedom doesn't matter — it fails because it misrepresents what is happening. This is not freedom versus paternalism. It is engineered compulsion dressed in the language of choice.

And for the children and adolescents who are the most severely affected — the argument doesn't even get that far. Minors cannot meaningfully consent to being subjects of the largest behavioral modification experiment in human history. The freedom defense, applied to them, is not a serious position. It is a talking point generated by the legal and communications departments of very large companies with a lot at stake.

· · ·

Weizenbaum wrote about the compulsive programmer as a cautionary case — a clinical edge condition, a warning sign about where the relationship between humans and machines could go wrong. I don't think he imagined, even in his most alarmed moments, that the pathology would scale the way it has.

Three billion people. Many of them checking their phones within minutes of waking up. Many of them feeling a phantom vibration in their pocket when the phone isn't even there — a psychosomatic symptom of dependency that, in any other context, we would recognize immediately as what it is. Many of them genuinely unable to get through a meal or a conversation or a stretch of quiet without reaching for it.

This is not a moral failure of three billion individuals. It is the designed outcome of systems built by a small number of companies and optimized relentlessly, over more than a decade, to produce exactly this result.

Weizenbaum's compulsive programmer needed help. He also needed someone to ask: what have we built that makes this possible, and should we have built it?

We are long past due for that question. We are asking it now, imperfectly, in regulatory hearings and public health advisories and the quiet, worried conversations of parents who have watched something happen to their children and don't quite have the words for it.

I have words for it. We built a machine for manufacturing compulsion, sold it to the world as connection, and then acted surprised when people couldn't put it down.

Now we have to figure out what to do about it.
