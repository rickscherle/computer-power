Part I

What We Built and Why

I spent decades building things I didn't fully think through. I don't say that with false modesty. I was good at what I did. But being good at building something is not the same as being wise about whether to build it, or what it might become, or what it would cost people who had no say in the matter.

Most of the people I worked with were in the same position. We were engineers and programmers and system designers, and the culture we lived in rewarded us for solving the problem in front of us. It did not particularly reward us for asking whether solving that problem might create a larger one. That question — if it got asked at all — tended to come later. Usually after the system was already deployed. Often after the damage was done.

Part One is my attempt to understand how that happened. Not in the abstract, not as a critique of some other industry — but honestly, from the inside, from someone who was there.

The first chapter looks at what I'm calling the Great Delegation: the long, largely invisible process by which we handed decision-making authority over human lives to algorithmic systems, without anything resembling a public conversation about whether that was a good idea. The second chapter asks why it was so easy — why the efficiency argument was so seductive that it kept working even when the evidence of harm was accumulating. The third chapter is the one that's most personal for me, because it's about people I knew: the compulsive programmers Weizenbaum described, and what happened when the psychological patterns he identified in a small subculture got deliberately industrialized and deployed on three billion people.

What we built reflects what we valued. That's the uncomfortable truth at the center of these three chapters. Not what we said we valued. What we actually valued, revealed by the choices we made when the pressure was on and the money was flowing and nobody was asking hard questions.

I was part of this. I want to be honest about that before I start criticizing it.
