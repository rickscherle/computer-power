Part One  ·  Chapter One

The Great Delegation

Nobody voted on this.

I want to be clear about that, because it's easy to assume that a change this large must have had some formal moment of decision — a law passed, a commission formed, a public debate conducted and resolved. It didn't. At some point in the last thirty years, we handed over some of the most consequential decisions in human life — who gets bail, who gets a loan, who gets a job, which family gets investigated for child abuse — to algorithms. And we did it without anyone really asking whether we should.

I was in the room, or close to it, for a lot of this. I watched the systems get built. I watched the arguments get made. The arguments were not crazy. That's the part that still bothers me.

The pitch went something like this: humans are inconsistent. Humans are biased. Humans get tired and hungry and make different decisions on Monday morning than they do on Friday afternoon. A well-designed system, by contrast, applies the same rules every time, to every case, without fatigue or prejudice or mood. Surely that's better? Surely consistency is at least a step in the right direction?

It sounded reasonable. It still sounds reasonable, in the abstract. The problem is what happened when it left the abstract and landed in the real world.

· · ·

Take COMPAS. It stands for Correctional Offender Management Profiling for Alternative Sanctions — a name designed, I think, to make you stop reading before you get to the part that matters. COMPAS is a piece of software that has been used in American courtrooms to assess how likely a defendant is to reoffend. Judges consult its output when they're deciding whether to grant bail, what sentence to recommend, whether to allow parole. The system produces a score between one and ten.

A person's freedom, in part, depends on that number.

Here's what gets me. COMPAS is proprietary. Its algorithm is a trade secret. The defendant doesn't get to know how the score was calculated. The defense attorney doesn't know. The judge doesn't know. In 2016, ProPublica analyzed thousands of COMPAS scores and found that the system flagged Black defendants as future criminals at nearly twice the rate it flagged white defendants — and that it was wrong in both directions more than it was right. The company that makes it disputed the methodology. The courts have largely continued using it.

I think about that a lot. A person sits in a courtroom, their life genuinely at stake, and somewhere in the room there is a number that nobody can explain, generated by a process nobody is allowed to examine, that is influencing what happens to them. And we call this an improvement on human judgment.

Maybe it is, in some narrow statistical sense. That's what makes it so hard to argue with. But statistical improvement is not the only thing that matters in a courtroom. Something else happens there — or should happen there — that has nothing to do with prediction. A human being who has done something wrong stands before a community and is judged. That judgment has moral weight. It requires a human being willing to bear the responsibility of making it. You can't delegate that weight to a number. You can only hide it.

· · ·

COMPAS is not an isolated case. I wish it were.

Amazon built a hiring algorithm trained on a decade of their own recruitment data. The system taught itself, faithfully and efficiently, to replicate what Amazon's hiring managers had done in the past. What they had done in the past, it turned out, was hire mostly men. So the algorithm downgraded applications from women. Not because anyone told it to. Because that's what the data said success looked like. Amazon discovered this and quietly shut the system down in 2018. They didn't make a big announcement about it.

Healthcare algorithms have been found to systematically underestimate the medical needs of Black patients — not because they were programmed with racial bias, but because they used healthcare costs as a proxy for healthcare needs, and Black patients had historically received less care, so they looked, to the system, like they needed less. The system learned from the inequity and reproduced it as a recommendation.

Child welfare algorithms in multiple states and countries assess which families to flag for investigation. The variables they use — prior contact with social services, housing instability, benefit claims — are all correlated with poverty. Which means they're correlated with race. Which means poor and minority families get flagged at higher rates, investigated more often, subjected to more scrutiny, not because they harm their children more, but because they're more visible to the administrative systems the algorithm was trained on.

I've been watching this pattern for thirty years and it still surprises me. Not that it happens — I understand why it happens. What surprises me is how consistently we act surprised when it does. As if we didn't build these systems. As if they arrived from somewhere else.

· · ·

Weizenbaum had a distinction he kept coming back to, and I think it's the most important thing in his book. He separated the question of what computers can't do from the question of what computers shouldn't do. The first category gets smaller every year. The second doesn't move.

A machine can process a defendant's criminal record and output a risk score. In a purely technical sense, it can do that. What it cannot do is stand before that defendant and say: I have thought about what you did, and what brought you to it, and what your life has been, and what it might yet be, and this is my judgment. It cannot be argued with. It cannot be shamed. It cannot change its mind because something in the room moved it. These aren't temporary limitations we'll engineer our way past. They're what a machine is.

And some decisions — I believe this with everything in me — require exactly that. Not prediction. Judgment. Not calculation. Moral presence. The willingness of one human being to look at another and say: I am responsible for this decision, and I will carry it.

When we delegate those decisions to systems, the moral weight doesn't disappear. It just gets spread so thin, across so many engineers and executives and procurement officers and regulators, that nobody feels it anymore. That's not justice. That's the bureaucratic management of the appearance of justice.

· · ·

I want to be fair to the other side of this, because the other side has a real point.

Human judgment is not some pure, uncontaminated thing we're protecting from the machines. I know that. I've seen the research. A 2011 study of Israeli parole boards found that the probability of a favorable ruling dropped from about sixty-five percent at the start of a session to nearly zero right before a food break — then bounced back after lunch. Not because the cases changed. Because the judges were hungry. That's not a story about a few bad judges. That's a story about the limits of human cognition under real-world conditions.

And there are domains where I think algorithmic judgment is genuinely better, and where using it is not just defensible but morally required. Screening medical images for early signs of disease. Flagging financial fraud. Identifying sepsis in hospital patients before the symptoms become obvious. These are tasks where the algorithm is faster, more consistent, and demonstrably more accurate than the human alternative — and where the stakes are high enough that using an inferior tool out of some sentimental attachment to human process would be its own kind of moral failure.

So I'm not against the technology. I've spent my life building with it and marveling at it. What I'm against is the assumption that because it works well in those domains, it works well everywhere — or that the only question worth asking is whether it improves the numbers.

In the courtroom, at the bedside, in the welfare office, the numbers are not the only thing that matters. The process matters. Who is accountable matters. Whether there is a human being in the room who owns the decision — who can be questioned, who can be appealed to, who can be wrong and know it — matters in a way that is not captured by any metric I know of.

· · ·

Here is the thing that keeps me up at night. Nobody decided to do this. Not really. There was no moment when society gathered and said: we have considered the question of whether to delegate judgment over human lives to algorithmic systems, and we have decided yes.

It happened the way most large things happen. A bank saved money on loan officers. A court system needed to manage an overwhelming caseload. A hospital wanted to allocate nursing resources more efficiently. Each decision made local sense. Nobody was sitting in a room thinking about the aggregate. Nobody was asking: what kind of civilization do we become if we do this at scale?

Weizenbaum asked that question in 1976. He was largely ignored. The people building the systems didn't want to hear it, and the people affected by the systems didn't know to ask it, and here we are.

The question was never asked. It still can be. That's why I wrote this book.
