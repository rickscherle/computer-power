Part III

The Domains We Must Not Surrender

At some point in every argument like this one, you have to stop talking in general terms and say specifically what you mean.

I've made the case that we've handed too much over to machines. I've argued that the efficiency argument is more seductive than it is sound. I've described the accountability vacuum and the illusion of understanding and the engineering of compulsion. But a reasonable reader has every right to push back and say: okay, but which decisions exactly? Where do you draw the line? How do you distinguish the cases where automation is genuinely beneficial from the cases where it isn't?

Those are fair questions. They deserve specific answers, not philosophical gestures.

Part Three is where I try to give them. I've chosen four domains — justice, care, democratic life, and warfare — not because they're the only ones that matter, but because they're the clearest cases. These are the places where I think the argument is hardest to dismiss, where the harm of delegation is most concrete, and where the reasons for keeping humans in the loop go deeper than efficiency or accuracy.

The argument I make in each case is the same in structure. It's not just that the systems might get the answer wrong — though they do. It's that in these domains, the process is part of the point. A trial is not just a mechanism for producing accurate verdicts. Care is not just a mechanism for delivering optimal outcomes. Democracy is not just a mechanism for aggregating preferences. Warfare is not just a mechanism for eliminating threats. In each case, the human encounter at the center of the thing — the confrontation, the relationship, the decision made by a person who will live with its consequences — is morally significant in itself. You can't automate it away without changing what it is.

I believe that. I want to show you why.
