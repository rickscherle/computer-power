Part II

The Illusion of Understanding

Here is something I have watched happen many times, in many contexts, and it never stops being strange.

You show someone a system that produces convincing outputs — fluent text, confident recommendations, plausible-sounding explanations — and they start treating it as if it understands what it's doing. Not because they've been told it does. Not because they've examined it carefully and concluded that it does. Just because it looks like it does. The appearance is enough.

Weizenbaum called this the ELIZA effect, after his own chatbot — a program so simple it embarrasses me to describe it, and yet people poured their hearts out to it. They knew it was a program. They still responded as if something on the other end was listening.

I used to think this was a problem specific to naive users, people who didn't know enough about how these systems worked to maintain appropriate skepticism. Then I watched it happen to engineers. To researchers. To people who had built systems very much like the ones they were anthropomorphizing. The knowledge of how the trick worked did not reliably protect you from falling for it.

Part Two is about that trick and what it costs us. The first chapter traces the ELIZA effect from a laboratory experiment to a civilizational condition — what it means that billions of people now interact daily with systems that produce the appearance of understanding without the reality. The second chapter examines what I think is an underappreciated danger: not that these systems are biased or inaccurate, but that they are opaque — that we have built decision-making infrastructure that nobody, including the people who built it, can fully explain. The third chapter takes on the hardest question, the one I find genuinely unsettling even after decades of thinking about it: with systems as sophisticated as the ones we have now, does the distinction between simulating understanding and actually understanding still hold? And if it does — how do we defend it?

I think the distinction holds. But I understand why people find it harder to see than they used to. That difficulty is itself part of the problem.
