Part V

A Different Path

I know what you're expecting here. The part where the book pivots to solutions and the solutions turn out to be modest and hedged and slightly disappointing relative to the scale of the problem that's been described. I've read enough books like this one to know that pivot is coming, and I've always found it a little deflating.

I'm going to try to be honest with you instead.

The problems described in this book are large. They are structurally embedded in institutions and industries that have enormous resources and very little incentive to change. The people who would benefit most from change are, in many cases, the people with the least power to demand it. And the pace at which the technology evolves has, so far, consistently outrun the pace at which societies can deliberate about it and respond.

I don't think there's a policy fix that resolves all of this. I don't think there's a technical solution — the people who built the problems are not going to engineer their way out of them. I don't even think public awareness, by itself, is sufficient, though it's necessary.

What I do believe — and this is the thing I've come back to again and again across the years of thinking that produced this book — is that the most important battles are not primarily technical or regulatory. They are about what we refuse. What we decide, as individuals and as institutions, we will not do regardless of whether we can. What lines we hold even when holding them is inconvenient. What it means to say: this is a decision that must be made by a human being who will own it, and no algorithm is going to substitute for that, and I don't care how accurate the system is.

Part Five is about that refusal. What it looks like in practice. Where it's already happening. What it would take to make it more common.

Weizenbaum ended his book with a call for conscience. Fifty years later, I'm making the same call. Not because I think it's sufficient. Because I think it's where everything else has to start.
